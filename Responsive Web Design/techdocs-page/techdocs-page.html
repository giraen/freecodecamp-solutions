Introduction

Arduino, a remarkable platform for electronics enthusiasts, beckons with its versatility and accessibility. As a microcontroller platform, Arduino acts as a gateway to transform imaginative concepts into tangible realities. Whether you're a curious beginner or a seasoned innovator, Arduino's open-ended nature welcomes a myriad of creative possibilities.

At its heart, Arduino boasts an array of microcontroller boards that facilitate seamless connections to sensors, motors, and diverse components, laying the foundation for a multitude of projects. From simple LED displays that catch the eye to intricate interactive systems that blur the line between art and technology, Arduino's adaptability knows no bounds.

Guided by its user-friendly interface and enriched by a thriving community, Arduino encourages you to embark on a journey into the realms of coding, electronics, and robotics. The platform's extensive collection of sensors, modules, and shields offers endless combinations to explore. Step into the world of Arduino, and unlock the potential to shape the future through innovation and hands-on learning.

Getting Started

Before heading out to programming with Arduino, you should first have the following:

  1. Arduino Boards (e.g. Arduino Uno, Arduino Mega, and Arduino Nano)
  2. Arduino IDE
  3. USB Cable
  4. Electronic Components (e.g. resistors, LEDs, and sensors)

In this documentation, we will assume that you uses Arduino Uno for reasons that it is often used by beginners in Arduino. Further, this documentation will only focus on the programming aspect of Arduino. Documentations, for instance, of Arduino boards' schematic diagram and other technical aspects will not be covered but you are welcome to visit Arduino's official website.
